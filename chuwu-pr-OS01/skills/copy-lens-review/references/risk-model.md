# Risk Model

Use a 100-point model. Higher scores mean higher public communication risk.

| Metric | Weight | What To Check |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Semantic ambiguity risk | 15 | Puns, metaphors, intimate terms, slang, subculture terms, or terms whose public meaning differs from internal intent |
| Value conflict risk | 20 | Gender, family ethics, generation, children, education, class, region, occupation, public morals, festival seriousness |
| Group offense risk | 20 | Whether a group may feel mocked, reduced, objectified, stigmatized, or used as a prop |
| Occasion mismatch risk | 15 | Whether brand, holiday, channel, product, and tone fit together |
| Screenshot spread risk | 10 | Whether the shortest cropped sentence becomes worse than the full context |
| Media amplification risk | 10 | Whether it can become a headline, public-value debate, or official-media critique |
| Brand asset damage risk | 10 | Whether controversy conflicts with long-term brand trust and requires costly explanation |

## Levels

- 0-20: low risk, can release
- 21-40: minor risk, revise lightly before release
- 41-60: medium risk, rewrite before release
- 61-80: high risk, pause release
- 81-100: crisis risk, do not release

## Scoring Guidance

Do not score by sensitive words alone. Add risk when multiple factors stack:

- A public holiday plus intimate terms.
- A family or gender identity plus a joke that can be read as mockery.
- A subculture term plus a mainstream channel.
- A short screenshotable hook plus a high-explanation intent.
- A good intention that cannot survive without explanation.

## Common High-Risk Patterns

- "Circle slang" placed in a broad public context.
- "Young and funny" tone used in solemn festivals or family ethics.
- Women, mothers, bodies, class, region, disability, disease, age, education, or occupation used as the joke surface.
- Copy that claims empowerment but narrates the group through another dominant group.
- A line that can be screenshotted into a harsher standalone meaning.

## High-Risk Narrative Patterns

Use these when explaining why a copy may fail even without offensive words:

**Romanticizing structural hardship**
Packaging real hardship (labor, illness, poverty, caregiving burden) as "poetic," "free," or "dream-chasing." The gap between idealized framing and public reality drives backlash.

**Exceptional case as representative**
One extreme success story implies broad achievability. Audiences test plausibility and may turn the story into mockery.

**Brand self-congratulation**
Copy reads like an internal report or achievement list. Readers cannot see "what does this have to do with me."

**Promises beyond reality**
Absolute claims about safety, quality, price fairness, or outcomes that the brand cannot consistently guarantee become evidence if an incident occurs.

**Group opposition triggers**
Copy provokes opposition between genders, classes, regions, occupations, or brand vs. individuals through comparison, implied unfairness, or one-sided stereotypes.

**Internal jargon barrier**
Company abbreviations, industry terms, or project codenames that ordinary readers cannot parse without explanation.
